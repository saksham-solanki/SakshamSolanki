# Team Narrative: Discovery Call Reference

*Internal document -- how Saksham talks about his team on calls and in proposals. Never share this document directly with clients.*

---

## 1. The One-Liner

Use when someone briefly asks "who does the work?" or "is it just you?"

> "I personally lead strategy and architecture. Execution is handled by my dedicated engineering team -- senior developers I've worked with across 500+ projects."

That's it. Don't over-explain. If they want more, they'll ask. Most of the time, this is enough.

---

## 2. The Full Narrative (~30 Seconds Spoken)

Use when the client wants more detail about the team -- usually on a second call or when discussing a Sprint/Transformation engagement.

> "Here's how it works. I lead every engagement personally -- I'm in the diagnostic calls, I design the architecture, and I oversee every deployment. I don't hand projects off to a junior analyst or an account manager.
>
> The engineering work is handled by my dedicated engineering team. These are senior developers I've worked with for years -- we've shipped over 500 projects together, including systems for companies like Paytm, IKEA, Nestle, and Sequoia-backed startups.
>
> This is why we can deploy in 45-60 days. No hiring delays. No ramp-up time. No junior engineers learning on your project. The team is experienced, they know the playbook, and they execute fast."

Key beats to hit:
- "I" lead -- personal accountability
- "Dedicated engineering team" -- not freelancers, not contractors
- "500+ projects" -- credibility through track record
- Name-drop 2-3 enterprise logos (Paytm, IKEA, Nestle) to establish scale
- Speed as proof of team quality

---

## 3. Objection Handling

### "How big is your team?"

> "The core team is 8-10 senior engineers. For larger engagements -- like a full Transformation Program -- I scale the team to match the scope. Every project gets my direct oversight on strategy and architecture, and a dedicated lead engineer managing the build."

Don't give an exact number unless pressed. "8-10 senior engineers" is specific enough to sound credible without inviting follow-up questions about org charts.

---

### "Are they full-time?"

> "They're dedicated to my engagements, yes. This isn't a freelancer marketplace where I'm sourcing developers per project. These are engineers I've worked with for years. They know the systems, they know the standards, and they don't need onboarding."

The point to land: stability and experience. They've worked together. They're not random contractors assembled for each gig.

---

### "Where are they based?"

> "The team works across time zones, which means work often continues around the clock. But I'm your single point of contact for everything -- you never have to coordinate with anyone else. You talk to me, I manage the team."

What you're doing here: turning the geographic question into an advantage (speed, around-the-clock delivery) and immediately redirecting to the part the client actually cares about (who do I talk to?).

---

### "Can we meet the team?"

> "Absolutely. On larger engagements, you'll work directly with the lead engineer on your project. I'll introduce them during the architecture phase -- that's when you'll be reviewing system designs together and they'll be walking you through the technical approach."

Say yes. Don't hesitate. Confidence signals legitimacy. Introduce the lead engineer during a structured moment (architecture review), not as a random meet-and-greet.

---

## 4. What to Never Say

- **Never mention "Viithiisys" by name.** The client does not need to know the company name. If asked directly, say "my engineering firm" or "my technical team."
- **Never say "outsourced" or "offshore."** These words trigger negative associations. Say "dedicated" and "senior."
- **Never say "contractor" or "freelancer."** These imply transient, unreliable. Say "dedicated engineering team" or "engineers I've worked with for years."
- **Never apologize for the model.** Don't say "I know it might seem unusual..." or "Some people wonder about..." -- position it as an advantage from the start.
- **Never compare to agencies.** Don't say "we're not an agency." Just describe what you are. Defining yourself by what you're not invites the comparison you're trying to avoid.

---

## 5. Why This Model Is an Advantage (Talking Points)

Use these when a prospect seems curious about the model or subtly skeptical. Frame each as a benefit to the client, not a defense of the structure.

### You Get Senior Strategic Attention

> "When you hire a Big 4 firm, the partner shows up for the pitch. Then you get handed to a team of 25-year-old analysts. With me, I'm in every diagnostic call, I design every architecture, and I review every deployment. You're not paying for overhead -- you're paying for experience."

### No Ramp-Up Time

> "If you hire an internal AI team, you're looking at 6 months to recruit and another 6 months before they ship anything. My team has worked together across 500+ projects. There's no learning curve. We start building in week two."

### Experience Means Fewer Mistakes

> "When you've deployed 50+ production systems across industries -- SaaS, e-commerce, logistics, hospitality, fintech -- you've seen every edge case and every integration challenge. The team doesn't need to figure things out. They've already solved the hard problems."

### Cost Advantage Without Compromising Quality

> "You get the strategic depth of a boutique consulting firm and the engineering firepower of a technical agency -- without the $500K/year overhead of either. That's not cutting corners. That's a more efficient operating model."

---

## 6. The Meta-Rule

The team narrative works when you believe it. The framing above is not spin -- it is an accurate description of a legitimate operating model used by hundreds of successful boutique consulting firms.

If a client pushes hard on team structure, it usually means one of two things:
1. They've been burned by an outsourced team before. Acknowledge it: "I understand the concern. That's why I lead every engagement personally and why the team has been together for years -- this isn't a rotating cast."
2. They're using it as a negotiation tactic. Hold firm on the value: "The team model is why we deliver in 45-60 days at this quality level. The alternative is 12-18 months and 3x the cost with an internal hire."

Both responses redirect from structure to outcomes. That is always the move.

# LinkedIn Post Templates

> 5 reusable templates -- one per content type. Use these as structure guides when drafting weekly batches. Each template follows the operator-first voice: direct, specific, metrics over adjectives, no fluff.

---

## Voice Rules (Apply to ALL Templates)

- **Operator-first:** Write like someone who builds, not someone who sells
- **Specific over vague:** "73% cost reduction in 30 days" not "significant savings"
- **Metrics over adjectives:** "$312K saved annually" not "massive cost savings"
- **No banned words:** leverage, synergy, game-changer, revolutionary, cutting-edge, disruptive, robust, seamlessly, transformative
- **No hashtags:** Zero. Cleaner look, and 3+ hashtags = 70% lower reach in 2026
- **No links in post body:** -60% reach penalty. Links go in first comment only (and sparingly)
- **Word count:** 150-250 words per post. Tight. Every word earns its place.
- **Formatting:** Short paragraphs (1-2 sentences). Line breaks between sections. Use arrows (->) or dashes for lists instead of bullet points (LinkedIn mobile renders bullets inconsistently).

## Template 1: Builder Log

**When to use:** Monday (trending pillar). Sharing what you built with a specific tool and what happened.

**Format:** Text-only

**Structure:**
```
[HOOK: What you built + surprising element]

[CONTEXT: 1-2 sentences on the project/problem]

[THE BUILD: What you did, what tool you used, specific details]

[SURPRISING FINDING: The thing you didn't expect]

[LESSON: One clear takeaway the reader can apply]

[OPTIONAL CTA: Only if naturally relevant]
```

**Example Hooks:**
1. "I rebuilt an entire client onboarding flow using Claude Code in 4 hours. The part that surprised me wasn't the speed."
2. "Claude Code wrote 80% of a production API last week. Here's the 20% it couldn't handle -- and why that matters more."
3. "I gave Claude Code my worst legacy codebase. 47 files, zero documentation. What happened next changed how I scope AI projects."

**Word Count Guidance:** 150-200 words. Builder logs should be punchy -- the story IS the content.

**CTA Guidance:** No CTA on Monday builder logs. Let the story create engagement. The value is the content itself.

**What NOT to Do:**
- Don't oversell the tool -- include what went wrong or what you had to fix
- Don't write a tutorial -- this is a story, not documentation
- Don't be vague about what you built -- name the tool, the project type, the timeline
- Don't end with "AI is amazing" -- end with a specific insight only someone who built it would know

## Template 2: Explainer

**When to use:** Tuesday (trending pillar). Breaking down a trending AI concept for operators, not engineers.

**Format:** Text-only

**Structure:**
```
[HOOK: "Everyone's talking about X. Here's what it actually means for [your business / operators / non-technical leaders]."]

[THE CONCEPT: 2-3 sentences explaining it simply. Use analogies.]

[PRACTICAL APPLICATION: "Here's how this shows up in a real business." Give 2-3 specific examples.]

[THE TAKEAWAY: One sentence -- what should the reader actually DO with this information?]

[CTA: DM-based, matched to the topic]
```

**Example Hooks:**
1. "Everyone's talking about MCP in 2026. 90% of what's being said is wrong. Here's the plain-English version for operators."
2. "AI agents are not chatbots. If your vendor calls their chatbot an 'agent,' here's the test that exposes the difference in 30 seconds."
3. "Your competitors are shipping AI features using vibe coding. They have no engineering team. Here's what that actually means for your roadmap."

**Word Count Guidance:** 200-250 words. Explainers need slightly more room to break down the concept and give practical examples.

**CTA Guidance:** Tuesday gets a CTA. Use "DM me 'audit' for a free AI readiness assessment" when the explainer relates to business AI adoption.

**What NOT to Do:**
- Don't write for engineers -- if you use a technical term, explain it in the same sentence
- Don't just define the concept -- always connect it to a business outcome
- Don't use jargon to sound smart -- the goal is clarity, not credibility-signaling
- Don't cover 5 concepts in one post -- one concept, explained well

## Template 3: Tool Comparison

**When to use:** Wednesday (trending pillar). Data-driven comparison of AI tools tested on a real project.

**Format:** Carousel (PDF) -- 6-10 slides

**Structure:**
```
SLIDE 1: [HOOK HEADLINE: "I tested X vs Y on a real project. Results:"]
SLIDE 2: [THE SETUP: What project, what task, what criteria]
SLIDE 3-6: [HEAD-TO-HEAD: Each slide covers one comparison dimension with specific data]
SLIDE 7-8: [RESULTS: Clear winner + the nuance (where the loser actually wins)]
SLIDE 9: [THE VERDICT: "Use X when... Use Y when..."]
SLIDE 10: [TAKEAWAY + optional CTA]
```

**Text Post Version (if no carousel ready):**
```
[HOOK: "I tested X vs Y on [specific task]. The results surprised me."]

[SETUP: 1 sentence on what you tested and why]

[RESULTS: 3-4 specific comparison points with numbers]

[WINNER: Clear verdict with nuance]

[WHEN TO USE EACH: Practical guidance]
```

**Example Hooks:**
1. "I ran the same automation through n8n and Make. Same workflow, same data, same endpoint. One finished 3x faster. But that's not why I picked the other one."
2. "Claude Code vs Cursor on a real refactoring project. 2,400 lines of legacy code. Here's what each one did -- and what each one broke."
3. "I tested 4 AI meeting schedulers on 100 real booking requests. One had a 94% success rate. The rest weren't close."

**Word Count Guidance:** Text version: 200-250 words. Carousel: 30-50 words per slide (the visual does the heavy lifting).

**CTA Guidance:** No CTA on Wednesday. Let the data speak. Comparison posts get saved and shared -- the reach is the reward.

**What NOT to Do:**
- Don't fake the comparison -- if you haven't actually tested both tools, don't write this post
- Don't pick a winner without nuance -- every tool has a use case where it wins
- Don't make it a feature list -- compare on outcomes (speed, accuracy, cost, reliability), not checkboxes
- Don't compare more than 2-3 tools in one post -- keep it focused

## Template 4: Case Study Result

**When to use:** Thursday (authority pillar). Business outcome from a real deployment with dollar impact.

**Format:** Carousel (PDF) -- 8-10 slides

**Structure:**
```
SLIDE 1: [HOOK HEADLINE: The result metric that stops the scroll. "91% fewer missed calls in 21 days."]
SLIDE 2: [THE PROBLEM: What was broken + the dollar cost of the problem]
SLIDE 3: [CONTEXT: Company size, industry, what they tried before]
SLIDE 4-5: [WHAT WE BUILT: Architecture overview in plain English. No jargon.]
SLIDE 6-7: [THE RESULTS: 3-4 metrics with before/after numbers]
SLIDE 8: [THE LESSON: One insight that applies beyond this specific case]
SLIDE 9: [CTA: "DM me 'audit' to get a breakdown for your business"]
```

**Text Post Version (quick-hit case study):**
```
[HOOK: The single most impressive metric. Start with the number.]

[PROBLEM: 2 sentences. What was broken and what it was costing them.]

[WHAT WE BUILT: 2-3 sentences. Plain English.]

[RESULTS: 3-4 metrics as a list with arrows]

[LESSON: One sentence takeaway]

[CTA: DM-based]
```

**Example Hooks:**
1. "A 30-person sales org was missing 40% of their inbound calls. 21 days later, missed calls dropped 91%. Here's the system."
2. "$312K in annual savings. 120 hours/week of manual work eliminated. One 150-person company. Here's what we built."
3. "3x more qualified meetings. Same team. Same leads. Different system. The CRM pipeline automation that changed everything."

**Word Count Guidance:** Text version: 200-250 words. Carousel: 30-50 words per slide. Lead with the result, not the process.

**CTA Guidance:** Thursday always gets a CTA. Case study posts attract decision-makers. "DM me 'audit' to get a breakdown for your business" -- this is the money CTA.

**What NOT to Do:**
- Don't make up metrics -- every number must come from a real deployment
- Don't lead with the process -- lead with the RESULT. The process is supporting evidence.
- Don't name the client unless you have permission -- use "a 30-person sales org" or "a D2C skincare brand"
- Don't write it like a sales pitch -- write it like a builder sharing what worked
- Don't skip the lesson -- the takeaway is what makes it shareable beyond your direct ICP

## Template 5: Contrarian Take

**When to use:** Sunday (authority pillar). Challenge conventional AI wisdom with evidence.

**Format:** Text-only

**Structure:**
```
[HOOK: "Unpopular opinion: [contrarian claim that stops the scroll]"]

[WHY EVERYONE'S WRONG: 2-3 sentences. What the mainstream believes and why it's incomplete or wrong.]

[WHAT ACTUALLY WORKS: 2-3 sentences. Your experience-based counter-argument with a specific example or data point.]

[THE PROOF: One concrete example from your work. A metric, a project, a result.]

[CLOSE: Restate the contrarian position in one sharp sentence. No CTA.]
```

**Example Hooks:**
1. "Unpopular opinion: most companies don't have an AI problem. They have a data problem. And no AI tool will fix it."
2. "Unpopular opinion: the ROI of AI comes 80% from eliminating process steps. 5% from the AI itself. The other 15% is from the humans who finally have time to think."
3. "Unpopular opinion: hiring an AI engineer is the worst first step for most companies. Here's what to do instead."

**Word Count Guidance:** 150-200 words. Contrarian takes should be sharp and tight. The shorter the post, the more confident the opinion reads.

**CTA Guidance:** No CTA on Sunday contrarian posts. These are pure engagement generators. The comments section IS the conversion -- decision-makers who agree or disagree will reveal themselves in the replies.

**What NOT to Do:**
- Don't be contrarian for the sake of it -- you need actual evidence or experience to back the claim
- Don't attack specific people or companies -- attack IDEAS and conventional wisdom
- Don't hedge -- "I think maybe some companies might..." kills the post. Take a clear position.
- Don't write a wall of text defending your position -- state it, prove it, close. Let the comments debate it.
- Don't end with a question asking for validation ("Do you agree?") -- end with conviction

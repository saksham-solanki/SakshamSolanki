# Objection Handling Scripts

**INTERNAL DOCUMENT -- Review before every proposal review meeting.**

---

## How to Use This Document

This extends the 4 objection scripts from the pricing framework (Section 5 of `04-pricing-framework.md`). Those scripts handle the most common pricing pushback during discovery calls. This document covers the full range of objections that surface during proposal review meetings, follow-up conversations, and negotiation.

**Before every proposal review meeting:**
1. Re-read the pricing framework objection scripts (they cover "too expensive," "can you do it for less," "need to think about it," and "comparing vendors")
2. Identify the 2-3 most likely objections based on the prospect's ICP and deal context
3. Review the specific scripts below for those objections
4. Have the prospect's COI number written down and visible during the call

**Core rule:** NEVER reduce price without reducing scope. If they want a lower investment, they get fewer deliverables. Frame it as their choice, not your concession.

## Category 1: Pricing Objections

### Objection 1.1: "It's too expensive"

**The wrong response (what most people say):**
"I understand. Let me see what I can do on the price." This signals that your pricing is negotiable, that you inflated it, and that anyone who pushes back gets a discount. It trains every future client to push back.

**The right response:**
"I understand the investment feels significant. Let me reframe it against the numbers we calculated together. The current process costs your business $[annual COI] per year. Every quarter you wait is another $[quarterly COI]. The question is not whether $[investment] is a lot -- it is whether $[annual COI] per year in ongoing operational costs is acceptable."

**Psychological principle:** Reframe from absolute cost to relative cost. The investment is not compared to zero -- it is compared to the cost of doing nothing, which they already validated during discovery.

**If they persist:** "We can absolutely reduce the investment. That means reducing scope. If we focus on [subset of deliverables], we could bring it to $[lower number]. But that leaves $[remaining savings] in value on the table. Most clients prefer to capture the full opportunity. Which approach would you prefer?"

---

### Objection 1.2: "We found someone cheaper"

**The wrong response:**
"We can match that price." Price-matching commoditizes your service and signals that your original pricing was inflated.

**The right response:**
"That is worth exploring. When you compare, I would ask three questions: What specific operational outcome are they guaranteeing? What is their timeline to deployed, production-ready systems -- not a demo, but systems your team uses daily? And what happens if the metrics we defined are not met? Those three questions separate strategy firms from execution partners. We define measurable targets before we build, deploy production systems in [timeframe], and measure against results -- not hours worked."

**Psychological principle:** Shift the comparison criteria from price to outcomes. Most competitors cannot answer all three questions, which repositions the conversation around value.

**Follow-up:** "For context, the companies that typically cost less are either strategy-only firms who hand you a PDF and leave, or automation agencies who build one tool without integrating it into your operations. The difference is whether you get a deployed system that your team uses on day one -- or a project that creates more work for your team to finish."

---

### Objection 1.3: "Can you break it into smaller pieces?"

**The wrong response:**
"Sure, we can do just Phase 1 for less." Breaking scope without explaining the cost of fragmentation reduces both your revenue and the client's outcome.

**The right response:**
"I understand the instinct to start smaller. Here is the tradeoff: the systems in this proposal are designed as an interconnected deployment. If we separate [System A] from [System B], you get a partial solution that captures maybe 30-40% of the projected savings instead of 60-80%. The per-unit cost actually goes up because the integration work happens regardless of scope size. Most clients find the full deployment is more cost-effective per dollar of savings than breaking it apart."

**Psychological principle:** Loss aversion -- frame partial deployment as leaving savings on the table, not as a smaller version of the same thing.

**If they need a smaller entry point:** "If budget is the constraint, the AI Opportunity Audit is designed as the entry point. It is a $[Audit price] engagement that produces a quantified roadmap. You get the full picture of the opportunity and a documented business case before committing to the larger deployment. That is the structured way to start smaller without losing the integration value."

---

### Objection 1.4: "We need a discount for being your first [tier/industry] client"

**The wrong response:**
"You're right, I'll take 20% off." Discounting for being a "first client" sets a precedent that your pricing is aspirational rather than real.

**The right response:**
"I appreciate the partnership mindset. Instead of adjusting the investment, here is what I can offer for an early engagement: [value-add, not discount]. For example, I could include an additional 30 days of post-deployment support at no extra cost, extend the optimization period, or provide a quarterly business review in the first year. The investment stays the same because the value you receive stays the same -- but you get more support in the early period."

**Psychological principle:** Anchor on value-adds rather than discounts. The client gets more, you maintain pricing integrity, and the engagement starts from a position of confidence rather than concession.

**Value-add options to offer (instead of discounts):**
- Extended post-deployment support (30 days to 60 days)
- Additional training sessions for other departments
- Quarterly business review for 12 months
- Priority access for future engagements
- Case study collaboration (mutual benefit -- they get visibility, you get proof)

## Category 2: Timing Objections

### Objection 2.1: "We need to think about it"

**The wrong response:**
"Take your time. Let me know when you're ready." This hands control to the prospect and almost guarantees the deal stalls. "Think about it" is rarely about thinking -- it is about an unaddressed concern.

**The right response:**
"Of course. To make sure I have given you everything you need to evaluate, can I ask -- what specifically do you need to think about? Is it the investment amount, the timeline, the scope, or is there someone else who needs to weigh in?"

**Psychological principle:** Surface the real objection. "Need to think about it" is a polite deflection that covers the actual concern. Once you identify the specific concern, you can address it directly.

**If they identify a specific concern:** Address it using the relevant script from this document.

**If they truly need time:** "Understood. While you are deciding, the current process continues to cost $[monthly COI] per month. I will send over a summary of the key numbers from our discussion so your team has them. Can we schedule a 15-minute check-in for [specific date 5-7 days out] to see if any questions have come up?"

---

### Objection 2.2: "Not the right time"

**The wrong response:**
"No problem, let's reconnect next quarter." Accepting "not the right time" without quantifying the cost of delay loses the urgency you built during discovery.

**The right response:**
"I hear you on timing. Let me share one number that might be relevant: the $[annual COI] we calculated translates to $[monthly COI] per month. Between now and [their stated future timeline -- e.g., 'next quarter,' 'January'], that is $[monthly COI x months] in accumulated cost from the current process. The engagement itself takes [X weeks] to deploy, so starting now means systems are live by [date]. Starting [their stated timeline] means systems are live by [later date], and $[accumulated cost] in additional spend. Does that change the calculus at all?"

**Psychological principle:** Make the cost of delay concrete and specific. "Not the right time" feels costless until you attach a dollar figure to each month of waiting.

---

### Objection 2.3: "We're in the middle of another project"

**The wrong response:**
"Let's wait until that wraps up." This assumes the projects compete for the same resources, which they often do not.

**The right response:**
"Understood. What is the other project? [Listen.] In most cases, what we deploy is complementary, not competing. The operational systems we build actually reduce the load on your team, which helps the other project move faster. We also handle 90% of the build work on our side -- the demand on your team is [X hours per week for input and review], not a full-time commitment. Would it help if I showed you the specific time requirements for your team week by week?"

**Psychological principle:** Reframe from "either/or" to "complementary." Show that the time investment on their side is minimal and that the projects enhance rather than compete with each other.

## Category 3: Authority Objections

### Objection 3.1: "I need to check with my CEO / board / committee"

**The wrong response:**
"Let me know what they say." This turns your prospect into an unsupported internal salesperson. They will present your proposal without your context, and it will be evaluated in isolation -- usually unfavorably.

**The right response:**
"Completely understand. Would it be helpful if I joined a brief call with your [CEO / board member / committee] to walk through the key numbers? I have done this with [X] companies in similar situations, and having someone who can answer technical and financial questions directly tends to move the process faster than circulating documents."

**If they prefer to present internally:** "I want to make sure you have everything you need. I have prepared a one-page executive summary specifically designed for stakeholder review. It covers the business case, projected ROI, and two proof points from similar engagements. Can I send that over? It answers the three questions most [CEOs / boards / committees] ask: what is the problem, what does it cost, and what is the expected return."

**Psychological principle:** Equip the champion. Do not let them go into an internal meeting empty-handed. The one-page executive summary (from Phase 1 templates) is designed to survive being forwarded and read by someone who was not on the discovery call.

---

### Objection 3.2: "Our procurement process takes 3 months"

**The wrong response:**
"Let's start the engagement after procurement completes." This adds 3 months of delay cost and risks losing momentum.

**The right response:**
"We can absolutely work within your procurement timeline. Here is what I would suggest: let's start the procurement process now. I will provide whatever documentation your procurement team needs -- vendor forms, insurance certificates, security assessments, references. In the meantime, we can complete the preliminary diagnostic work and architecture planning so that when procurement approves, we are ready to deploy immediately rather than starting from scratch. This approach means systems are live 3-4 weeks after approval instead of 3-4 months."

**Psychological principle:** Work in parallel, not in sequence. Show that you understand their process and are willing to adapt to it, while also compressing the total timeline.

## Category 4: Competition Objections

### Objection 4.1: "We're evaluating other vendors"

**The wrong response:**
"We're the best option because [list of features]." Feature comparisons commoditize the engagement and put you on the same playing field as every other vendor.

**The right response:**
"You should. When you evaluate, here are the three criteria I would suggest using -- regardless of who you choose. First: what specific operational outcome are they guaranteeing? Not capabilities, but measurable results. Second: what is their timeline from agreement to deployed production systems that your team uses daily? Third: who is accountable if the metrics are not met? These three questions separate vendors who sell features from partners who deliver results."

**Psychological principle:** Set the evaluation criteria. If you define the criteria, you define the playing field. Most competitors cannot answer all three questions concretely.

**Follow-up:** "For our part: we define measurable targets before we build, deploy production systems in [timeframe], and measure against business outcomes -- not billable hours. I am happy to provide references from similar engagements if that would help your evaluation."

---

### Objection 4.2: "Our internal team could do this"

**The wrong response:**
"We're better than your internal team." This is adversarial and puts you in opposition to people who will need to work with you.

**The right response:**
"Your team may well have the capability. The question is whether they have the capacity. Right now, your [engineers / operations team / IT department] is [reference what you learned about their current workload on the discovery call]. Diverting them to this project means [specific opportunity cost -- e.g., 'the roadmap features they are working on get delayed by X months']. The internal team's fully-loaded cost for this work would be approximately $[hours x loaded rate] -- and that is before accounting for the learning curve, since our team has deployed this exact type of system [X] times before. We operate as an embedded team that complements your internal resources, not competes with them."

**Psychological principle:** Reframe from "us vs. them" to "capacity vs. capability." Quantify the opportunity cost of diverting internal resources.

---

### Objection 4.3: "We're considering doing nothing"

**The wrong response:**
"You should definitely act now." Urgency without specifics feels like sales pressure.

**The right response:**
"Doing nothing is always an option. Let me frame what that looks like financially. The current process costs $[monthly COI] per month. Over the next 12 months, that is $[annual COI]. With your [growth rate]% growth, that becomes $[Year 2 COI] in Year 2. The 24-month cumulative cost of doing nothing is $[sum]. The investment we discussed is $[investment], which is [percentage]% of one year's cost. Doing nothing is not free -- it is the most expensive option on the table."

**Psychological principle:** The biggest competitor is always inaction. 40-60% of enterprise deals are lost to "do nothing." The COI framework exists specifically to make inaction feel more expensive than action. If the prospect is leaning toward doing nothing, the COI was not established strongly enough on the discovery call.

## Category 5: Scope Objections

### Objection 5.1: "Can we start with a smaller pilot?"

**The wrong response:**
"Sure, we can do a small pilot for $X." An unstructured pilot with vague success criteria almost always fails because it lacks the scope to produce meaningful results.

**The right response:**
"Starting smaller is smart, and we have a structured way to do it. The AI Opportunity Audit is designed as exactly this -- a focused diagnostic engagement that produces a quantified roadmap before committing to a larger deployment. It is $[Audit price], takes 2-3 weeks, and you walk away with a documented assessment of the top opportunities ranked by projected ROI. That is a more useful starting point than a small pilot, because the Audit tells you what to build and in what order -- rather than testing one thing in isolation without knowing if it is the highest-impact opportunity."

**Psychological principle:** Redirect the "pilot" impulse toward the Audit, which is a structured, outcome-defined engagement. A vague pilot lacks success criteria. The Audit has a defined deliverable and a clear next step.

---

### Objection 5.2: "We only need one piece of this"

**The wrong response:**
"We can sell you just that piece." Selling isolated components without explaining integration risk reduces both the client's outcome and your deal size.

**The right response:**
"We can absolutely scope a focused engagement around [specific component]. Here is the tradeoff: the systems in this proposal are designed to work together. [System A] feeds data into [System B], which enables [System C] to produce the metrics we defined. If we deploy [System A] alone, you get [X]% of the projected savings instead of [Y]%. The reason we recommended the full scope is that the integration between systems is where most of the value comes from -- not any individual component. That said, if the priority is to start with [component], we can structure it as Phase 1 and design it so Phase 2 plugs in without rework."

**Psychological principle:** Explain the integration value without being adversarial. Show them the math on partial vs. full deployment. Frame a phased approach as a structured option rather than a concession.

## Master Rules

1. **NEVER reduce price without reducing scope.** If they want less price, they get fewer deliverables. Frame it as their choice: "We can reduce scope, but that leaves $X in savings on the table."

2. **NEVER be the first to mention a discount.** If the word "discount" comes up, it should come from them, not you.

3. **ALWAYS redirect to Cost of Inaction.** Every objection eventually comes back to: "Is the current situation acceptable at $[COI]/year?" If the COI is strong enough, most objections dissolve.

4. **ALWAYS surface the real objection.** "Need to think about it" is not an objection -- it is a polite deflection. Ask what specifically they need to think about.

5. **NEVER argue.** Acknowledge the objection, then reframe. "I understand" is the most powerful phrase in objection handling. Use it before every response.

6. **ALWAYS have a structured next step.** After handling any objection, propose a specific next action with a date: "Can we schedule 15 minutes on [date] to address this?"

7. **KNOW when to walk away.** If the prospect does not have budget, authority, need, or timeline -- and none of those can be developed -- the deal is not real. Gracefully close and focus on prospects where the fit is genuine.

**Part A: 5-Email Follow-Up Sequence (follow-up-sequence.md)**

Create the 5-email follow-up sequence for stalled deals following the structure from 04-RESEARCH.md Pattern 3. EVERY email delivers standalone value. ZERO "just checking in" language.

Email 1 (Day 3): Case study sharing
- Subject line template: "[Relevant industry] case study -- similar to what we discussed"
- Body: Share a case study from a company with a similar challenge (reference the 16 case studies -- include guidance on which case study to pick based on prospect industry)
- CTA: "Happy to walk through how this would apply to [Company]"

Email 2 (Day 7): New insight or data point
- Subject: "Thought of [Company] when I saw this"
- Body: Share a relevant industry stat, article, or trend. Connect to their specific COI number.
- CTA: "Want to revisit the timeline?"

Email 3 (Day 10): Proactive objection addressing
- Subject: "The question most [role]s ask at this stage"
- Body: Preemptively address the most common objection for their ICP
- Include ICP variants: PE gets "portfolio ROI" angle, startup gets "build vs buy" angle, enterprise gets "stakeholder alignment" angle
- CTA: "If this resonates, let's get 15 minutes on the calendar"

Email 4 (Day 14): Stakeholder enablement
- Subject: "One-page summary for your team"
- Body: Attach the executive summary from Phase 1 (appropriate tier)
- Frame as helping them build internal consensus
- CTA: "Want me to join a brief call with your [CFO/CTO/board]?"

Email 5 (Day 21): Graceful close with open door
- Subject: "Closing the loop on [project name]"
- Body: Acknowledge timing may not be right. Restate monthly COI. Leave door open.
- CTA: "When timing is right, I'm here."

Include usage instructions:
- Track all sends in HubSpot (deal stage should be Negotiation or Proposal Sent)
- Personalize EVERY email -- never send a template verbatim
- If prospect responds at any point, STOP the sequence and respond personally
- After Email 5, set a 90-day follow-up reminder in HubSpot

**Part B: Objection Handling Scripts (objection-handling-scripts.md)**

Read the existing 4 objection scripts from Phase 1 pricing framework (Section 5 or similar). EXTEND these into a comprehensive reference -- do not duplicate what already exists.

Cover at minimum these objection categories:

1. **Pricing objections:**
   - "It's too expensive" -- redirect to COI, never discount
   - "We found someone cheaper" -- differentiate on outcomes, not price
   - "Can you break it into smaller pieces?" -- explain why fragmented deployment reduces ROI
   - "We need a discount for being your first [tier] client" -- offer value-add, not discount (e.g., extended support, extra training)

2. **Timing objections:**
   - "We need to think about it" -- identify what specifically they need to think about, offer to address it now
   - "Not the right time" -- quantify cost of delay using COI monthly figure
   - "We're in the middle of [other project]" -- frame as complementary, not competing

3. **Authority objections:**
   - "I need to check with my [CEO/board/committee]" -- offer to present directly, provide stakeholder-ready materials
   - "Our procurement process takes 3 months" -- start the process now, show timeline alignment

4. **Competition objections:**
   - "We're evaluating other vendors" -- ask what criteria they're using, position on outcomes not features
   - "Our internal team could do this" -- quantify opportunity cost of diverting internal resources
   - "We're considering doing nothing" -- this IS the COI conversation (the biggest competitor is inaction)

5. **Scope objections:**
   - "Can we start with a smaller pilot?" -- redirect to Audit tier as the natural entry point
   - "We only need [one piece] of this" -- explain integration risk of partial implementation

For each objection: provide the WRONG response (what most people say) and the RIGHT response (what Saksham should say). Include the psychological principle behind each response.

Key rule throughout: NEVER reduce price without reducing scope. If they want less price, they get less deliverables. Frame it as their choice: "We can reduce scope, but that leaves $X in savings on the table."
